Affordable Housing & Tenant Rights

To combat artificial demand and absentee speculation, this bill would limit foreign nationals to the purchase of only one residential property in the United States, and only for personal use.

Key provisions include:
  • Foreign nationals may own one residential unit only, no bulk buying, no real estate hoarding.
  • Residential properties left vacant for more than 180 days by foreign owners would incur a federal vacancy tax.
  • Corporations or shell entities linked to non-residents would be prohibited from purchasing additional units, and individuals linked to any such entity shall be precluded from owning any property.

This proposal directly reduces speculative demand, curbs gentrification pressure, and makes more housing available and affordable to the people who actually live and work in our communities.

This legislation would establish national baseline protections for all renters, especially in properties receiving federal subsidies or tax incentives.

Key rights include:
  • Right to Safe, Habitable Housing
    • Enforceable federal standards for maintenance, pests, air quality, mold, heating, and water access.
    • Emergency federal funding for urgent “livability” repairs and utility access in under-resourced communities.
  • Right to Just-Cause Eviction Protections
    • Landlords must provide legitimate cause (e.g., non-payment, lease violation) for eviction.
    • Ends arbitrary or retaliatory evictions.
  • Right to Lease Renewal Protections
    • Tenants in good standing cannot be denied renewal without just cause.
    • Ensures housing stability in rapidly gentrifying or high-demand neighborhoods.
  • Right to Counsel in Eviction Proceedings
    • Federal funding to guarantee legal representation for low- and middle-income tenants.
    • Modeled on successful city-level programs like New York City’s Right to Counsel law.
  • Right to Reasonable Rent Increases
    • Federally capped rent increases on subsidized units (e.g., CPI + 3% annually).
    • Enables cities and states to build on this floor with stronger protections.
  • Right to Anti-Discrimination Protections
    • Nationwide ban on source-of-income discrimination (e.g., against Section 8 voucher holders).
    • Enhanced enforcement of existing Fair Housing laws.
  • Right to Organize and Collective Bargain
    • Federal protections for tenant unions and associations against retaliation.
    • Support for collective bargaining on rent, maintenance, and building conditions.
  • Right to Transparency
    • Tenants must be informed of the actual owners and financial interests behind their landlords.
    • Mandatory disclosure of rent history, prior code violations, and government subsidies.

Corporate Greed & Excess Wealth

For decades, multinational corporations and ultra-wealthy individuals have extracted billions in profits from our communities while contributing less and less to the public good. It’s time to end this extraction economy and rewrite the tax code to work for working people and ensure that companies benefiting from America’s workforce and infrastructure are paying their fair share to sustain it.

I will push for a comprehensive overhaul of the corporate tax code that:

  • Eliminates tax loopholes and offshore shelters.
  • Enforces a minimum effective tax rate for large corporations.
  • Prevents corporations from deducting stock buybacks, lobbying expenses, or executive bonuses from their taxes.
  • Requires public reporting of corporate tax liabilities and effective tax rates.

The current income-based tax system lets the ultra-wealthy off the hook. Many of the richest Americans report little to no income, despite controlling hundreds of millions to billions in assets. They use those assets as collateral to borrow at low interest rates, avoiding income taxes entirely. This isn’t a loophole, it’s a design failure.

We propose a Ultra-Wealthy tax equal to 1% plus the previous year’s GDP growth, as reported by a federal index (such as the Bureau of Economic Analysis). For example, if GDP grew by 2%, the tax would be 3% on total net worth for individuals with over $100 million in assets.

This is not radical, it’s a modest and responsive mechanism based on how the ultra-wealthy actually build wealth: faster than the economy itself. This tax simply ensures that when the country grows, those who benefit most also help sustain it.

  • Stocks, real estate, private equity, art, yachts, and trusts.
  • Shell company holdings.
  • Assets leveraged for loans, whether technically “owned” or “borrowed against.”

Real Revenue Gains

The top 0.01% of U.S. households control over $18 trillion in wealth. Assuming an average GDP growth of 2–3%, the effective tax rate would hover around 3–4% annually, generating:

  • $540–720 billion per year, conservatively.
  • As much as $900 billion+ in strong economic years.

This revenue could fully fund or dramatically expand:

  • Medicare for All
  • Tuition-free public college and vocational programs
  • National affordable housing construction
  • Green energy and climate resilience projects
  • Universal pre-K and childcare access
  • Modernized public transit systems

All without raising taxes on 99.9% of Americans and potentially lowering them!

This isn’t about punishment, it’s about preservation. If democracy is to survive, those who have gained the most from it must begin to invest back into it. This tax is a first step toward rebalancing a system that has long asked everything from working people and almost nothing from the top.

No Tax Dollars for Genocide

I will not vote for any legislation—regardless of which party presents it—unless it includes clear and enforceable provisions to immediately end all economic and military aid to Israel and any other nation suspected of committing genocide, ethnic cleansing, or violations of international law.

No U.S. tax dollars shall go to any foreign government that stands in breach of these principles. The United States must finally end its complicity in war crimes and systemic oppression by halting all financial and military support to nations that violate fundamental human rights.

Beyond these red lines, I will oppose all foreign military aid, even to traditional allies, until we have guaranteed the following for the American people:

  • Universal, affordable healthcare
  • Accessible, affordable childcare
  • Tuition-free higher education for any student who cannot afford it

Ceasefire demands in regions like Gaza are the bare minimum, but they are not enough. We need binding legal prohibitions on military aid that are conditioned on both domestic justice and international accountability.

I recognize that this approach may unsettle some allies, but there are multilateral institutions such as NATO to fill temporary gaps. The truth is, America must finally confront its toxic dependence on the military-industrial complex, a dependency that fuels war, destabilizes regions, and enriches defense contractors at the expense of human life.

We must also confront the political stranglehold that prevents accountability. At the heart of this impasse lies a false binary perpetuated by the Zionist political establishment: “Our survival requires your complicity, and refusing that complicity is antisemitic.” That framing is undemocratic and dangerously manipulative.

The reality is this: Congress will never defund Israel directly, not while it's effectively owned by foreign lobbying interests. The only viable path is to abolish foreign military aid entirely—no loopholes, no exceptions, full detachment.

We face a choice: support our allies blindly, or stop being complicit in genocide. Choosing complicity not only violates our conscience, it corrodes the very foundation of American democracy. If we fund atrocities, we forfeit the right to speak of liberty, justice, or dignity. In that case, the American project has failed.

This transformation is about more than war, it’s about economic justice and structural repair. It calls out our second addiction: a capitalist model of endless extraction, which demands rising profits no matter the cost in human life. This system has gutted the Global South, sustained neocolonialism, and created a global class of indentured labor.

If we want to solve the immigration crisis, we must stop enabling the economic conditions that displace millions. The first step: end the funneling of American resources into foreign militaries and exploitative regimes.

This policy is unapologetically simple: Take care of Americans first. Stop funding violence abroad. Prioritize people over power. Replace performative patriotism with real justice.

A Broken Health System

While universal health care remains the ultimate goal, and may eventually require a shift beyond privatization to a nationalized system, we need immediate, tangible wins. Too many Americans are currently denied essential care, and urgent reforms are necessary to restore fairness, dignity, and access.

For now, we must acknowledge that health insurance will continue to exist as a private enterprise. But it cannot remain one that profits by denying care. To rebalance the system, I will introduce legislation to legally cap the allowable profit margin of private health insurance companies at 1.5% of total annual revenue, with a firm 2% ceiling, beyond which all excess profit is redirected to national healthcare trust funds.

For now, we must acknowledge that health insurance will continue to exist as a private enterprise. But it cannot remain one that profits by denying care. To rebalance the system, I will introduce legislation to legally cap the allowable profit margin of private health insurance companies at 1.5% of total annual revenue, with a firm 2% ceiling, beyond which all excess profit is redirected to national healthcare trust funds.

Some may claim this sounds un-American, illegal, or even antithetical to capitalism. But it is none of those things. In fact, we already tolerate and regulate monopolies in essential services i.e. electricity, water, gas, even cable, despite the foundational illegality of monopolies in a free market. Why? Because as a society, we recognize that in certain sectors, unrestricted competition can undermine the public good. Health care is no different, except that its consequences are even more immediate and deadly.

To be American is to believe in markets, but also to recognize that when the private sector threatens the public good, it is both legal and moral to apply the least restrictive measures needed to protect people. A modest cap on profit is one such measure, and it’s long overdue.

Importantly, this proposal is not radical. Most major insurers already operate within this range. Industry profit margins typically fall between 1% and 4%, with many earning around 1.5% to 3%. This plan doesn’t dismantle the business model, it simply formalizes what we already know is sustainable, and aligns it with the public interest.


This reform would:

  • Keep insurers financially viable and competitive
  • Prevent profiteering in life-and-death decisions
  • Promote care-driven business models over denial-based strategies
  • Balance shareholder interests with a moral obligation to patients

As it stands, the insurance industry too often functions more like a societal parasite than a collective ally, siphoning money from suffering rather than preventing it. If this system is going to remain private, it must act in the interest of the public good. A fair cap on profits is not just common sense, it’s a small but vital step toward a healthcare system that finally works for everyone.

Empowering Labor & Wage Earners

America’s economy is built on the backs of working people, yet workers have virtually no say in how major corporations are run. It’s time to change that. I will introduce a bill requiring legally mandated representation of non-executive, non-management employees on the boards of directors of all publicly traded companies, modeled after the doctrine of co-determination in countries like Germany.

This proposal is the most immediate, tangible, and accessible labor reform within today’s political climate, giving working people a seat at the table where decisions about wages, layoffs, safety, and job security are actually made.

Whether or not the bill passes, it will do something powerful: force every member of Congress to go on the record, to show whether they are truly committed to uplifting the working class or merely paying lip service. This is about transparency, accountability, and shifting the balance of power toward those who do the work, not just those who reap the rewards.

Corporate executives should have to look the very people they affect in the eye. Decisions about mass layoffs, stagnant wages, or speed-up policies feel different when the voices of workers are right there in the room. A disgruntled workforce is an inefficient workforce, and even worse is a fearful one. Shared power breeds better outcomes for morale, productivity, and justice alike.

And if corporations are to be treated as people under the law, then we must recognize that their soul is not found solely in the corner office, but in the collective heartbeat of their wage-earning workers. Any structure that excludes those workers from power is not only unjust, it is inhuman.

This isn’t about punishing success, it’s about protecting the people who make that success possible.

Overturn Citizens United & End Dark Money

The Supreme Court’s Citizens United decision transformed our democracy into an open auction. In 2010, the Court ruled that corporations and other outside groups have the same First Amendment rights as individuals when it comes to political spending.

The result was threefold:

  • Unlimited contributions: Donors—whether individuals, corporations, or unions—could give unlimited amounts of money to influence elections.
  • Direct corporate political spending: Corporations and other artificial entities gained the explicit right to spend directly on political advocacy, treating their treasury funds as “speech.”
  • Hidden donor identities: Much of this spending could be routed through nonprofits and shell organizations, allowing donors to remain anonymous to the public.

This ruling unleashed a flood of dark money that has warped the priorities of Congress, creating a political class more enamored with celebrity status than public service, and reserving meaningful access to those with immense wealth.

This is a profound breach of the unspoken social contract: that elected representatives act as fiduciaries of the public trust, required to exercise power and allocate resources solely for the benefit of the people, not as brokers selling that power to the highest bidder. Today, the benefits of that trust flow almost entirely in one direction: toward the consolidation of wealth, the deepening of political inequality, and the entrenchment of a permanent ruling class.

I will fight for a constitutional amendment to overturn Citizens United, establish full transparency in all political contributions, and eliminate dark money from federal elections. This means:

  • Mandatory real-time disclosure of every dollar spent to influence an election, including the ultimate, original source of funds.
  • Closing the 501(c)(4) loophole that enables corporations and foreign interests to launder donations through opaque nonprofits.
  • Strict campaign spending limits to prevent oligarchs from treating candidates like personal assets.

Our democracy cannot endure if electoral outcomes are determined by wealth rather than the will of the people. The federal government must once again be the beneficiary of the people’s trust, not the pawn of those who can afford to purchase it.

Break Monopolies, Lower Everyday Costs

The affordability crisis isn’t bad luck, it’s the result of powerful corporations cornering markets, controlling supply chains, and setting prices with no real competition. When a handful of companies dominate an industry, prices go up, quality goes down, and small businesses get pushed out.

We see it everywhere: meatpacking giants driving up the cost of beef and chicken, an egg industry dominated by a few producers charging record prices during shortages, seed and pesticide monopolies like Monsanto keeping farmers dependent and prices high, and pharmaceutical companies blocking cheaper generics to protect their profits. This is why groceries cost more, why gas spikes without warning, and why prescription drugs are unaffordable for millions.

For farmers, monopolies are an existential threat. A small number of agribusiness giants now control the markets for seeds, feed, fertilizer, and the processors who buy their crops and livestock. This means farmers often have only one or two buyers to sell to and those buyers dictate the price. At the same time, monopolistic suppliers hike the cost of essential inputs, locking farmers into debt and dependency. It’s a squeeze from both ends: they’re paid less for what they produce, and they pay more for what they need to keep producing. This system destroys family farms, hollows out rural economies, and forces the next generation off the land.

Breaking up monopolies isn’t about punishing success it’s about making sure no company can strangle competition or squeeze working Americans, from grocery shoppers to the farmers who feed them.

Congress needs to join the fight, not leave it to regulators alone. I will push for legislation to:

  • Ban mergers that make already-dominant corporations even bigger.
  • Break up companies that inflate prices or lock out competition.
  • Stop vertical integration in critical sectors like food, energy, and tech that allows one company to control every step from production to sale.
  • Give states more power to go after price-fixing and anti-competitive behavior without waiting for Washington.

History shows that when monopolies are broken up, consumers benefit. When the U.S. government forced the breakup of Standard Oil in 1911, energy prices fell, and innovation surged. When AT&T’s telephone monopoly was dismantled in 1984, competition drove down long-distance rates and fueled the telecom revolution. More recently, actions against tech giants and agribusiness monopolies have shown that challenging concentrated corporate power leads to lower costs and more choice. If we want lower prices on essentials like food, fuel, and medicine—and if we want farmers to earn a fair living for honest work, we must restore real competition and take the power back from the corporate giants who’ve rigged the market in their favor.
